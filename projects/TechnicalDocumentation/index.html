Introduction

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small non-coding RNAs that regulate gene expression at the post-transcriptional level. These tiny regulators play a crucial role in various biological processes, including brain development and neuronal plasticity.

In this webpage, we will explore how miRNAs might be related to alcohol consumption during adolescence and how this interaction can impact the adolescent brain during this critical developmental stage.

What are MicroRNAs

MicroRNAs are approximately 22-nucleotide-long RNA molecules that interact with messenger RNA (mRNA) to regulate gene expression. By binding to specific sequences on mRNA, they can suppress translation or induce degradation, thereby influencing the amount of proteins produced. MicroRNAs have a broad range of gene regulation and are involved in a variety of cellular and physiological processes.

O'Brien, J., Hayder, H., Zayed, Y., & Peng, C. (2018). Overview of microRNA biogenesis, mechanisms of actions, and circulation. Frontiers in endocrinology, 9, 402.

Adolescent Brain Development

During adolescence, the brain undergoes significant changes in structure and function. Neuronal development and the maturation of brain circuits are particularly sensitive to environmental influences, including alcohol consumption. At this stage, the brain remains malleable and vulnerable to potential harm.

Spear, L. P. (2013). Adolescent neurodevelopment. Journal of adolescent health, 52(2), S7-S13.

During brain development, miRNAs fine-tune the expression of genes that are critical for the formation of neural circuits and the establishment of functional connections between neurons. Dysregulation of miRNAs during development can lead to abnormal brain wiring and impair cognitive and behavioral functions. MicroRNAs are intricately involved in various stages of neural development:

  • Neurogenesis
  • Neuronal migration
  • Axon guidance and synaptogenesis
  • Neuronal maturation and plasticity
  • Neural survival and apoptosis
Rao, Y. S., & Pak, T. R. (2016). microRNAs and the adolescent brain: filling the knowledge gap. Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, 70, 313-322.

MicroRNAs and Alcohol Consumption

Recent research has shown that alcohol consumption can affect the expression of certain microRNAs in the adolescent brain. These changes in microRNA expression could have implications for the gene regulation of pathways associated with alcohol consumption and addiction.

For instance, some studies have identified microRNAs that are altered in response to alcohol consumption and are related to the modulation of dopamine, a neurotransmitter involved in the brain's reward system.

Lim, Y., Beane-Ebel, J. E., Tanaka, Y., Ning, B., Husted, C. R., Henderson, D. C., ... & Zhang, H. (2021). Exploration of alcohol use disorder-associated brain miRNA–mRNA regulatory networks. Translational psychiatry, 11(1), 504.

MicroRNAs as Risk Biomarkers

Given that microRNAs respond specifically to environmental changes, including alcohol consumption, they have emerged as potential biomarkers for assessing the risk of developing alcohol-related problems during adolescence. By analyzing microRNA expression profiles in biological samples, such as brain tissue or blood serum, we may identify patterns that suggest susceptibility to developing alcohol addictions or consumption-related issues in later stages of life.

Lewis, S. A., Doratt, B., Sureshchandra, S., Pan, T., Gonzales, S. W., Shen, W., ... & Messaoudi, I. (2022). Profiling of extracellular vesicle‐bound miRNA to identify candidate biomarkers of chronic alcohol drinking in nonhuman primates. Alcoholism: Clinical and Experimental Research, 46(2), 221-231.

Conclusions

Research into microRNAs and their relationship with adolescent alcohol consumption remains an emerging field. As we gain a better understanding of the specific mechanisms involved in this interaction, we may develop more precise and effective therapeutic strategies for preventing and treating addiction problems during this critical stage of development.

Continued research is essential to unravel the complex interplay among microRNAs, alcohol consumption, and the adolescent brain.
